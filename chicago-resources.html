Step 1

Find an AA Meeting

AA meetings are free, confidential, and available every day across Cook County — in churches, community centers, online, and everywhere in between. There's no sign-up and you don't have to say anything your first time.

Open

Open Meetings

Anyone can attend — members, family, friends, or curious newcomers. No commitment needed.

Closed

Closed Meetings

For people who identify as alcoholic or think they may have a problem with alcohol.

Speaker

Speaker Meetings

One or two members share their personal story. Great for newcomers — mostly listening.

Step Study

Step Study

The group works through one of the 12 Steps together. More structure, more discussion.

Big Book

Big Book

Discussion meetings centered on readings from the AA text. Good for digging deeper.

Online

Online / Zoom

Available 24/7. Great for privacy, late nights, or if you can't leave home.

Step 2

Treatment Programs in Cook County

If meetings alone aren't enough, or if you need medical support first, these are real programs in or near Cook County. Most accept Medicaid and offer sliding-scale fees — cost shouldn't stop you from calling.

What's the difference between detox, residential, PHP, and IOP?
Detox — Medical monitoring while your body clears alcohol. Usually 3–7 days. Often the first step if there's physical dependence. Don't try to detox from alcohol alone — it can be dangerous.

Residential / Inpatient — You live at the facility, usually 28–90 days. Intensive, structured, and removes you from your environment. Best for people with severe addiction or unstable home situations.

PHP (Partial Hospitalization Program) — Treatment several hours a day, 5 days a week, but you go home at night. A step down from residential.

IOP (Intensive Outpatient Program) — Typically 3 days a week, 3 hours each session. More flexible — good if you're working or have family responsibilities.

Medication Support

Medication-Assisted Treatment (MAT)

MAT uses FDA-approved medications alongside counseling to treat alcohol and opioid use disorders. It's not "trading one addiction for another" — it's evidence-based medicine that significantly improves recovery outcomes. Many people in AA also use MAT.

What medications are used for alcohol use disorder?
Naltrexone (Vivitrol) — Reduces cravings and blocks the "reward" feeling from alcohol. Available as a daily pill or a monthly injection. No risk of dependence.

Acamprosate (Campral) — Helps reduce the discomfort of early sobriety by stabilizing brain chemistry. Taken 3x daily.

Disulfiram (Antabuse) — Causes an unpleasant physical reaction if you drink while taking it. Works best with strong motivation and support.

If You're Not Ready Yet

Harm Reduction Resources

Not everyone is ready for treatment right now — and that's real. Harm reduction meets people where they are. If someone is still using, keeping them alive and safer is the priority. Recovery can come later. These resources don't judge.

💉
Free Mail Delivery

Next Distro — Free Naloxone by Mail

Mail-order naloxone (Narcan) delivered directly to your door — no questions, no judgment. Available in most states for free. Naloxone reverses an opioid overdose within minutes and is easy to use as a nasal spray. Everyone who knows someone using opioids should have it.

nextdistro.org →

Chicago Recovery Alliance (CRA)

One of the oldest and most respected harm reduction organizations in the country, right here in Chicago. Distributes naloxone, fentanyl test strips, clean supplies, and provides referrals to treatment — without pressure or judgment.

NEXT Illinois — Fentanyl Test Strips
